Behind the Wayanad landslides: Extremely heavy rain, fragile ecology

Wayanad, a serene district in Kerala, has recently been devastated by a series of landslides. The death toll has alarmingly risen to 156, with many still trapped under the debris as rescue operations continue tirelessly. The landslides have been attributed to a combination of extremely heavy rainfall and the fragile ecology of the region.

The region has witnessed unprecedented rainfall over the past few weeks, leading to saturation of the soil and eventually causing massive landslides. The fragile ecology of the Western Ghats, exacerbated by deforestation, unplanned construction, and poor land management practices, has significantly contributed to the disaster.

The government has deployed several rescue teams, including the National Disaster Response Force (NDRF), to search for survivors and recover bodies. However, the challenging terrain and continuous rainfall are hampering the rescue efforts.

Experts have long warned about the dangers of unchecked development in ecologically sensitive regions like Wayanad. The need for sustainable practices and stricter enforcement of environmental regulations has never been more evident.

As the situation unfolds, the priority remains on rescuing those still trapped and providing relief to the affected families. The disaster serves as a grim reminder of the impact of climate change and the importance of preserving our natural environment.

LIVE Updates: The death toll continues to rise as rescue operations persist in the affected areas. The authorities have urged people to stay indoors and avoid unnecessary travel.
